04 Technical Solutions & Architectures

AI handover solutions span the protocol stack. They fall into structural low-latency mechanisms, prediction models, and learning-based control.

4.1 L1/L2-Triggered Mobility (LTM) — the low-latency substrate

LTM executes handover in three phases [1]:

Phase 1

Preparation

Network configures candidate target cells; UE sends L1 measurement reports for those cells.

Phase 2

Pre-synchronization

While still on the source, UE syncs to target DL and does RACH so the target has uplink timing (TA) ready.

Phase 3

Execution

A lightweight L1/L2 cell-switch command (with TA) triggers the switch — no full RRC reconfiguration → ~20–30 ms.

Because candidates are pre-prepared and timing is pre-acquired, LTM removes the heavy reconfiguration and random-access delay of L3 handover. Where AI enters: selecting/ranking candidate cells and predicting the optimal switch instant — the "when/where," which LTM itself leaves to network implementation. [3]

4.3 Learning-based control (reinforcement learning)

Rather than predicting and then applying fixed rules, RL directly learns a handover policy — mapping observed radio/mobility/load state to handover actions, optimizing a reward that balances throughput, failures, and ping-pong. Common formulations use Deep Q-Networks (DQN/DDQN) and policy-gradient / DDPG for continuous parameter tuning (hysteresis, time-to-trigger). Digital-twin and LSTM-aided variants are used to safely pre-train agents before live deployment. [9][10][11][13]

UE-side vs. network-side inference. A recurring architectural choice (visible in both research and patents): run the ML model in the UE (which then suggests a target via an enhanced measurement report / new event) or in the base station (which infers from UE-provided assistance info such as velocity, location, trajectory). Federated learning is proposed to train UE-side models without centralizing raw data. [18][20]
