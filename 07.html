01 Executive Summary

Handover — moving an active connection from one cell/beam to another as a device moves — is being reshaped by two convergent forces: lower-layer (L1/L2) triggered mobility (LTM) that slashes interruption time, and AI/ML that turns handover from a reactive, threshold-based decision into a predictive, proactive one.

Key takeaway. Standardized "AI handover" today is really two tracks running in parallel. (1) Structural low-latency handover (LTM) is fully specified and being commercialized — AI is not required for it but is the roadmap. (2) AI/ML mobility optimization (measurement/trajectory prediction, RL-based parameter tuning, proactive target selection) is partly standardized (3GPP TR 37.817 framework, Rel-19 study/normative items) and heavily active in research, vendor labs, O-RAN RIC apps, and patents — with 6G positioned as the point where prediction becomes native.

02 Background: Why Handover Needs AI

In legacy 5G NR (Rel-15) mobility, the UE reports Layer-3 filtered measurements (RSRP/RSRQ/SINR) of serving and neighbor cells; when a configured event (e.g., A3) fires, the network issues an RRC reconfiguration to hand the UE to a target cell. This L3 handover is robust but slow: reconfiguration and random access cause a connection interruption that can reach 50–90 ms, with significant signaling overhead. That is unacceptable for latency-critical XR, cloud gaming, remote control, and industrial automation. [1]

Two problems compound this in dense and high-frequency (mmWave) deployments: ping-pong (repeated back-and-forth handovers wasting signaling and hurting throughput) and handover failure / radio-link failure from mistimed or late handovers. Fixed thresholds and hand-tuned hysteresis/time-to-trigger cannot adapt to the huge diversity of speeds, trajectories, and load conditions. This is precisely the regime where machine learning — predicting signal evolution, UE trajectory, and load — offers gains. [8][12]

The two complementary levers

  • Latency lever — LTM: Move the handover trigger and execution down to L1/L2 with pre-configured candidate cells and pre-synchronization, cutting interruption to ~20–30 ms (and toward zero). Structural, deterministic.
  • Intelligence lever — AI/ML: Predict when and where to hand over (measurement/beam/trajectory prediction), and how to set parameters (RL tuning), reducing failures and ping-pong. Data-driven, adaptive.

Core KPIs handover research targets

  • Handover interruption time (ms of data-plane outage)
  • Handover failure rate (HOF) & radio-link failures
  • Ping-pong rate (unnecessary handovers)
  • Signaling overhead per successful handover
  • Throughput / spectral efficiency during mobility

03 Standardization Landscape (3GPP & O-RAN)

Handover evolution in 3GPP has proceeded release by release. AI/ML entered as a formal RAN framework in Rel-18 and expands in Rel-19, alongside the LTM low-latency handover machinery.

3GPP mobility & AI/ML release timeline

Rel-16 — CHO & DAPS2020
Conditional Handover (CHO): UE pre-receives target config and executes when a condition is met, decoupling decision from execution to cut failures. DAPS (Dual Active Protocol Stack) keeps the source link until the target is ready, for near-0 ms interruption in specific cases. [8]
Rel-17 — SON/MDT & mobility enhancements2022
Enhanced data collection (MDT), SON improvements, and mobility for higher frequencies — laying the data foundation for later ML.
Rel-18 — LTM + AI/ML for NG-RAN (5G-Advanced)Dec 2023
L1/L2-Triggered Mobility (LTM) standardized: candidate-cell preparation, pre-synchronization, L1-triggered cell switch → ~20–30 ms interruption. In parallel, AI/ML for NG-RAN normative work (built on TR 37.817 functional framework) covering three use cases: Mobility Optimization, Load Balancing, and Network Energy Saving. [1][2][4]
Rel-19 — Inter-gNB / Conditional LTM & AI/ML expansion2024–2025
Inter-gNB LTM (fast, seamless handover across base stations), Conditional LTM (CLTM) (LTM's low latency + CHO's resilience), and L1 measurement enhancements (event-triggered L1 reporting, CSI-RS based measurement). AI/ML for NG-RAN extends to network slicing, CCO, and mobility optimization for NR-DC. AI/ML for the air interface (RAN1) advances beam management, CSI, and positioning prediction. [3][5][6]
Rel-20 → 6G — AI-native mobility2026+
Predictive, "zero-latency" and "invisible" handover; AI anticipates user movement to hand over before degradation; unified terrestrial / non-terrestrial (NTN) mobility. Rel-20 studies frame the 6G on-ramp. [3][6]

O-RAN Alliance: where AI handover runs

O-RAN's RAN Intelligent Controller (RIC) gives AI handover a deployment home outside the 3GPP box:

  • Non-RT RIC + rApps (>1 s): policy/parameter optimization — e.g., mobility robustness optimization, handover parameter tuning via analytics/ML.
  • Near-RT RIC + xApps (10 ms–1 s): closed-loop control of handover/mobility decisions per UE via the E2 interface.

Recent work explores AI-driven multi-modal adaptive handover control as O-RAN apps. [16][17]

04 Technical Solutions & Architectures

AI handover solutions span the protocol stack. They fall into structural low-latency mechanisms, prediction models, and learning-based control.

4.1 L1/L2-Triggered Mobility (LTM) — the low-latency substrate

LTM executes handover in three phases [1]:

Phase 1

Preparation

Network configures candidate target cells; UE sends L1 measurement reports for those cells.

Phase 2

Pre-synchronization

While still on the source, UE syncs to target DL and does RACH so the target has uplink timing (TA) ready.

Phase 3

Execution

A lightweight L1/L2 cell-switch command (with TA) triggers the switch — no full RRC reconfiguration → ~20–30 ms.

Because candidates are pre-prepared and timing is pre-acquired, LTM removes the heavy reconfiguration and random-access delay of L3 handover. Where AI enters: selecting/ranking candidate cells and predicting the optimal switch instant — the "when/where," which LTM itself leaves to network implementation. [3]

4.2 Prediction-based mobility (the "AI" in AI handover)

ApproachWhat is predictedTypical modelBenefit

Beam prediction (mmWave)Best beam pair in time/spaceDL classifiers (RAN1 use case)Reduced beam-measurement overhead & failures
Prediction-based CHOWhether/when CHO condition will holdDeep learning on measurement historyRobust mmWave handover with fewer failures [15]

4.3 Learning-based control (reinforcement learning)

Rather than predicting and then applying fixed rules, RL directly learns a handover policy — mapping observed radio/mobility/load state to handover actions, optimizing a reward that balances throughput, failures, and ping-pong. Common formulations use Deep Q-Networks (DQN/DDQN) and policy-gradient / DDPG for continuous parameter tuning (hysteresis, time-to-trigger). Digital-twin and LSTM-aided variants are used to safely pre-train agents before live deployment. [9][10][11][13]

UE-side vs. network-side inference. A recurring architectural choice (visible in both research and patents): run the ML model in the UE (which then suggests a target via an enhanced measurement report / new event) or in the base station (which infers from UE-provided assistance info such as velocity, location, trajectory). Federated learning is proposed to train UE-side models without centralizing raw data. [18][20]

05 Vendor & Operator Progress

2025–2026 saw AI-RAN move from lab to live commercial trials. Handover-specific AI is mostly embedded within broader "AI-native RAN" and mobility portfolios. Each vendor below is treated separately, with its own source links at the end of the subsection. No major vendor sells a standalone "AI handover" product; handover intelligence is bundled into (a) LTM/mobility features in 5G-Advanced software and (b) AI-RAN / RIC automation platforms.

How to read "handover relevance." Some items below are direct (LTM, mobility optimization, beam-tracking for mobility). Others are adjacent — AI-RAN substrates (real-time neural inference, intelligent beam steering, autonomous-network engines) that AI handover decisions run on top of. Both are flagged honestly per vendor.

5.1  Ericsson Sweden Direct

Ericsson leads LTM standardization and frames the roadmap as "zero-latency handovers on the path to 6G," where AI anticipates a user's movement to hand over before signal degradation. It has launched an AI-native / AI-first RAN portfolio (2025–2026) and applies reinforcement learning to RAN algorithms (e.g., AI-based antenna tilting). With T-Mobile US it ran a world-first large-scale commercial trial of an AI-native Scheduler with Link Adaptation on live 5G-Advanced traffic (May 2026): ~10% spectral-efficiency and up to 15% downlink-throughput gains — the real-time-inference substrate future AI handover will use.

Sources: 5G-Advanced handover: LTM · Toward zero-latency handovers (Dec 2025) · Leveraging AI for RAN optimization · The RAN gets smarter (Jun 2026) · T-Mobile + Ericsson AI-RAN world-first (May 2026)

5.2  Nokia Finland Adjacent

Nokia is pursuing a distinct AI-RAN strategy (notably diverging from Ericsson's), centered on RAN automation and applying AI to mobility robustness, parameter optimization, and energy efficiency rather than a single named handover product. Its mobility intelligence surfaces through self-organizing/automation tooling and O-RAN-aligned RIC apps.

Sources: Light Reading — Ericsson & Nokia diverging on AI-RAN · TelecomLead — AI-first RAN & vendor share 2025

5.3  Huawei China Direct

At MWC 2025 (Mar 2025) Huawei launched AI-centric 5.5G (5G-Advanced / 5G-A) solutions to "ignite the mobile AI era," including intent-driven products GigaGear (multi-domain — time/frequency/space/power — resource scheduling for diverse users and AI scenarios), GreenPulse (multi-agent energy/spectral efficiency), and GainLeap built on the RAN Intelligent Service Engine (RISE). Huawei is driving Autonomous Network L3 → L4+ and is the largest 5G standard-essential-patent holder. With China Mobile it built a wireless autonomous network whose "Eyes/Brain/Hands" architecture automatically steers antenna beams toward congested areas — direct mobility/coverage optimization (see §5.7 for measured results).

Other reported results across the literature

  • Deep RL parameter optimization (DDPG): autonomous tuning of hysteresis/TTT reduces handover failures and ping-pong vs. fixed 3GPP settings across varied speeds. [11]

09 Outlook & Open Challenges

Where it's heading

  • Convergence of LTM + AI: LTM provides near-zero interruption; AI provides the predictive "when/where." Together → "invisible," proactive handover in 6G.
  • AI-native RAN: real-time inference in the RAN (proven at commercial scale in 2026) becomes the platform for per-UE handover control.
  • Cross-domain mobility: unified handover across terrestrial & non-terrestrial (satellite) networks.
  • Standardized model life-cycle: 3GPP/O-RAN converging on data collection, training, inference, and monitoring interfaces.

Open challenges

  • Generalization: models trained in one deployment/mobility regime may not transfer; real-world datasets (e.g., [28]) are scarce.
  • Explainability & safety: RL policies must avoid catastrophic handover failures; digital-twin pre-training helps.
  • UE energy & compute for on-device inference; signaling overhead of ML config.
  • Interoperability: multi-vendor model exchange, and privacy for UE trajectory data (→ federated learning).
